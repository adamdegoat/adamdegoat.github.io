Your property budget has three parts. Most buyers only count one.

I've watched buyers spend weeks falling for a home they couldn't actually afford — because they started browsing before working out three numbers. Sort these first, and you'll only ever look at places you can truly buy.

1 — What a bank will actually lend you. Your budget isn't just your savings; it's your savings plus what a bank will lend. That loan is capped by a rule based on how much of your monthly income already goes to repaying debt. So every car loan, credit-card balance or instalment you carry shrinks what you can borrow — clear those first and your budget quietly grows. Work out your max loan →

2 — The cash and CPF you'll need upfront. The down payment comes from two pockets: cash, and your CPF savings (the money set aside from your pay each month). For a bank loan, part of it must be paid in cash — CPF can cover much of the rest, but not all of it. If your cash savings are thin, this is the number that decides what's realistic for you.

Stamp duty and legal fees come out of your savings — not your loan.

3 — The costs on top of the price. Stamp duty is a government charge worked out on the higher of the price or the home's valuation, and it adds up fast. Add legal fees and a realistic renovation budget, and the cash you need on day one is well above the down payment alone. Estimate your stamp duty →
