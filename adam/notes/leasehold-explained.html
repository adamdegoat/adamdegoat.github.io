Is a 99-year leasehold a bad buy? The honest answer.

A lot of buyers hear the word “leasehold” and get nervous — like they're pouring money into something that just vanishes one day. Let me clear it up, plainly.

What it actually means: a leasehold home is yours for a set number of years — usually 99 — and after that, it returns to the government. Freehold means you own it for good, with no end date. Most homes in Singapore are leasehold, so if that's what you're looking at, you're in very normal company.

Here's the part that doesn't always get said out loud: a brand-new 99-year lease is a really long time — longer than most people stay in one home anyway. And leasehold usually costs less than a similar freehold nearby. So for a lot of buyers, it's a smart trade-off, not a downgrade.

A fresh 99-year home and one with 50 years left are completely different buys — even if they look identical.

What you should watch is old leasehold. As the years left run low, two quiet things happen: banks will lend you less, and you can use less of your CPF to pay for it. That makes a very old leasehold home harder to sell later, and its price can slip faster. So the number that really matters isn't “leasehold or freehold” — it's how many years are actually left.

So, the honest answer: leasehold isn't “bad.” Just always check the years remaining, and think about what that means for your loan, your CPF, and selling it again one day. Do that, and a 99-year home can be one of the smartest buys around.
