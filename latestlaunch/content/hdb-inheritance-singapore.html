Inheriting a Housing and Development Board (HDB) Flat

Inheriting an HDB flat involves complex legal considerations. Two key factors are whether the deceased had a valid will and whether the flat has other owners.

Religion and HDB Inheritance

Religion affects how HDB flats are inherited. For Muslim owners without a will, the flat is handled under Syariah Law Faraid. If there is a will, the owner can only bequeath 1/3rd of the property. The court distributes the remaining two-thirds according to Faraid rules.

For non-Muslim owners without a will, the Intestate Succession Act governs distribution. A relative may approach the court to be appointed as trustee. With a valid will, an executor oversees the process under the Probate and Administration Act.

Sole Ownership

Non-Muslim sole owners without wills have their flats sold, with proceeds divided: 50% to spouse and 50% equally among children. Without a spouse or children, parents inherit equally. Single owners with no family result in the flat reverting to the government.

Joint Tenancy

In joint tenancy, all co-owners hold equal shares. If one of the co-owners passes away, their interest is automatically passed to the remaining eligible owners. This occurs under the Right of Survivorship and overrides any will. Co-owners must be Singapore citizens or permanent residents, at least 21 years old.

Tenancy-in-Common

Under tenancy-in-common, each owner has a separate interest. If one of the owners passes away, their share of the ownership is transferred based on inheritance laws. It does not automatically transfer to remaining owners but follows the will or Intestate Succession Act.

The Process of HDB Inheritance

When a joint tenancy owner dies, the remaining owner must lodge a Notice of Death with the Singapore Land Authority (SLA), including the Death Certificate. The notice must be typed, printed, and witnessed by someone at least 21 years old.

For tenancy-in-common properties, the family should engage a lawyer to obtain a Grant of Probate. Without a will, a lawyer applies for a Grant of Letters of Administration. After obtaining court authority, the executor or administrator registers their legal right and applies to transfer the flat to new owners.

Beneficiary Already Owns Private Property

If the deceased bought the flat before August 30, 2010, the beneficiary may keep both properties, provided they are eligible and the family lives in the HDB flat.

If purchased on or after August 30, 2010, the beneficiary may keep only one property. Keeping the HDB flat requires selling the private property within six months. Keeping the private property requires selling the HDB flat within six months (or obtaining HDB assistance if minimum occupation period hasn't been met).

Beneficiary Already Owns Another HDB Flat

A person can only own one HDB flat at a time. If the beneficiary already owns an interest in an HDB flat, they will have to sell their interest in one of the two HDB flats. Keeping the inherited flat requires selling the other within six months. Keeping the existing flat requires selling the inherited flat within six months (or obtaining HDB assistance if minimum occupation period hasn't been met).

Beneficiary Already Owns Commercial Property

The beneficiary can keep the HDB flat if the commercial property has no residential component.

Minor Inheriting an HDB Flat

Minors can inherit HDB flats under a will or the Intestate Succession Act. However, minors lack legal capacity to contract for land sales. The surviving parent or guardian named in the will may manage the minor's inheritance until the child turns 21 years old.

Timeline for Selling After Death

A surviving joint tenant becomes the sole owner after lodging a Notice of Death with the SLA and may sell anytime, subject to Minimum Occupation Period (MOP) and other HDB requirements.

With a valid will, the executor must obtain a Grant of Probate before distributing the flat to beneficiaries, which takes time.

If there is no will, sections 37(1) and 37(4) of the Probate and Administration Act provide that the deceased's real and personal estate (including the HDB flat) will vest in the Public Trustee. Disputes or caveats may further delay the process, potentially requiring court intervention for contentious probate matters.
