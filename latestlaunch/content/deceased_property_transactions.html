Deceased Property Transactions: Guide on Property Transactions involving Deceased Owners

Upon a property owner's death, administrators or executors typically handle asset disposition. Several important legal considerations must be addressed before initiating a sale and purchase transaction.

What Happens Upon the Demise of a Person?

The initial question concerns who can administer the deceased's estate, depending on three scenarios: whether the property was held solely or jointly, whether a Will exists, or whether the person died intestate.

Manner of Holding

If property is held jointly with survivorship rights, surviving owners automatically inherit through the Rule of Survivorship. A Notice of Death must be filed with Singapore Land Authority with a certified death certificate copy. For tenants-in-common arrangements, survivorship doesn't apply, requiring Will or intestacy procedures instead.

The Deceased Had Drawn Up a Will

Named executors must obtain a Grant of Probate from Singapore courts. Once issued, executors can transfer property to beneficiaries or file a Transmission Application with SLA to become temporary proprietors for selling purposes—provided the Will contains explicit power-of-sale authority.

The Deceased Had NOT Drawn Up a Will

Administrators must obtain Letters of Administration. Section 18 of the Probate and Administration Act establishes priority categories: spouse, children, parents, siblings, nephews/nieces, grandparents, and uncles/aunts. Courts may appoint up to four administrators who must act unanimously.

Section 35(2), Conveyancing and Law of Property Act

The statute restricts estate property sales beyond six years post-death without court approval, "unless the sale or mortgage is made in pursuance of a power of sale or trust for sale."

Existing Mortgage

Death often triggers mortgage default events. Surviving co-owners typically must refinance rather than having loans recalled, creating challenges under strict banking debt-servicing requirements.

Conclusion

Estate property transactions require substantial forethought, particularly for sales. Legal consultation is essential for navigating these complex matters properly.
